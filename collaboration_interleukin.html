“Interleukin-23 Receptor and IBD”

Background: Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis are chronic episodic inflammatory disorders of the digestive tract. While they share many symptoms and exact a heavy toll from patients they are distinct diseases afflicting adults and children alike. The aetiology of these conditions is undefined, but is known to contain genetic, immunologic and environmental/behavioral components. Current treatments are essentially palliative and address generalized aspects of diet, inflammation control and flora at a whole body level. A great need exists to define and target better the immune functions that drive these conditions and to do that at the site of the problem, in the intestine and its associated immunological structures.

Recent discoveries: A new subset of T-cells has recently been discovered that promotes inflammatory responses aggressively, the “Th-17” cells. These cells secrete a cytokine, interleukin-17 (IL-17) that specifically upregulates inflammatory mediators, especially TNF. The main trigger of IL-17 secretion is yet another cytokine, interleukin-23 (IL-23). Little is known about the role of these two cytokines in IBD since they are recent discoveries themselves. However, sharp focus has been drawn on this system (IL-23 --> IL-17 --> TNF) by the discovery that a genetic mutant of the only receptor for IL-23 is tightly linked to Crohn’s disease. This is an exciting and important observation.

Opportunities for collaboration: We cannot realize the significance of our work without a critical scientific and clinical contribution from partnering physicians and other enthusiastic researchers who work in IBD. We would like to partner in two areas. First, with individuals who will provide us with clinical material of excellent diagnostic and treatment history. We cannot succeed without such partnerships. Second, we would like to open our laboratory to motivated individuals from within clinical and laboratory research to actively participate in our bench research. Such collaborations will connect basic researchers with clinical thinking in a hybrid academic / commercial environment.
